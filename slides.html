What is the CAC 40?

Context

  • The CAC 40 is France's benchmark stock market index — the 40 largest publicly traded companies on Euronext Paris
  • Think of it like the French equivalent of the S&P 500
  • Its composition changes quarterly as companies grow, shrink, or delist
Why does this matter?
Funds that track the CAC 40 must rebalance their holdings every quarter to stay aligned with the index. Getting this right — knowing which stocks to hold and in what quantities — is the core problem this project solves.

Index tracking

How Max Sharpe Ratio Works

Strategy B deep-dive

The core idea
Find the portfolio with the most return per unit of risk — not just the highest return.
Step 1 — Measure the past
Each stock's return and volatility over the previous quarter.
Step 2 — Find correlations
Stocks that don't move together lower the portfolio's combined risk.
Step 3 — Optimise
Find weights that maximise:
Sharpe = Return − Risk-free rate Volatility
Result
Weights that reflect the best risk-adjusted mix — not just company size.

Ledoit-Wolf Shrinkage

Making the optimisation reliable

The problem
~60 days of data to estimate 40 stocks. Small sample = noisy estimates.
Without shrinkage
Extreme values treated as real signals. Optimiser over-bets on noise.
Analogy
One rainy Tuesday ≠ it rains every Tuesday.
With Ledoit-Wolf
Pull extremes toward the average. Less sensitive to one quarter's fluke.
Result
Smoother covariance → weights that hold up next quarter, not just fit the past.

What PCA Does

Dimensionality reduction

The core idea
40 stocks don't move independently — they share common themes. PCA finds those themes and lets the optimiser work on them instead.
PC 1 — Broad market
Almost everything moves together when the market goes up or down. The first component captures this shared drift.
PC 2 — Sector rotation
Banks and industrials move differently from luxury and tech. The second component captures these sector tilts.
PC 3+ — Finer distinctions
Export-heavy vs. domestic, large cap vs. small cap, cyclical vs. defensive — each further component isolates a subtler pattern.
Auto-selecting k
We keep enough components to explain ≥90% of total variance. With a full year of history this is typically 5–12 factors. The rest is noise we deliberately discard.

How Stocks Get Their Weights

Factor → stock projection

Step 1 — Optimise in factor space
The MSR optimiser finds weights across the k factors, not across 40 stocks. Each factor weight says how much to bet on that particular market theme.
Step 2 — Project back via loadings
The loadings matrix (40 × k) records how much each stock participates in each factor. Multiplying factor weights by the loadings gives a raw weight for every stock.
wstocks = loadings × wfactors
Step 3 — Long-only constraint
Some projected weights come out negative (a stock with a negative loading on a high-weight factor). These are clipped to zero and the remainder renormalised to sum to 1.
Result
Stocks that participate strongly in the best-scoring factors get higher weights — naturally diversified across themes rather than concentrated in one or two names.

Known Limitation

Why the current weights look off

Data shortage
The price history on S3 was partially overwritten by rate-limited fetches. The restored file covers only ~3 months (84 rows) — giving just 3 monthly return observations after sampling.
Effect on PCA
With 3 observations and 39 stocks, the return matrix has rank 3 at most. PCA finds only k = 2 components that explain 100% of variance — not because 2 factors is correct, but because there isn't enough data to reveal more.
Effect on weights
Weights are computed from 3 data points. They are not robust and will look concentrated or arbitrary compared to what a full year of history would produce.
Fix in progress
A backfill of 274 missing trading days (2025-05-03 → present) is ready to run once Yahoo Finance rate limiting clears. With 12+ monthly observations, PCA will find 5–12 meaningful components and weights will be far more stable.
This is a data problem, not a strategy flaw
PCA-MSR is mathematically sound. The same approach on the full history would produce well-diversified, stable weights — as intended.

Max Sharpe Ratio Portfolio optimised

  • Sharpe ratio = return above the risk-free rate ÷ volatility — a measure of return per unit of risk
  • Uses historical returns to estimate expected return and covariance (how stocks move together)
  • Runs a numerical optimiser (scipy SLSQP) to find the weights that maximise the Sharpe ratio
  • Uses Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage (scikit-learn) to produce a more stable covariance estimate — avoids overfitting on short return histories

Step 1 — Dimensionality reduction
Run PCA on the 40-stock return matrix. Auto-select k components that explain ≥90% of variance (typically 5–12 for a year of history). Each component is a market factor — e.g. broad market, banks vs. energy, domestic vs. export.
Step 2 — Clean covariance
Fit Ledoit-Wolf on the k×k factor return matrix — far better conditioned than the 40×40 stock matrix with limited history.
Step 3 — Optimise in factor space
Run the same SLSQP MSR optimiser, but on k factors instead of 40 stocks. Weights represent factor exposures, not individual stocks.
Step 4 — Project back to stocks
Multiply factor weights by the PCA loadings matrix → individual stock weights. Long-only clip and renormalise.
vs Strategy B
More holdings, less concentration. The optimiser cannot over-bet on a single stock's idiosyncratic noise.
