What is the CAC 40?

Context

  • The CAC 40 is France's benchmark stock market index — the 40 largest publicly traded companies on Euronext Paris
  • Think of it like the French equivalent of the S&P 500
  • Its composition changes quarterly as companies grow, shrink, or delist
Why does this matter?
Funds that track the CAC 40 must rebalance their holdings every quarter to stay aligned with the index. Getting this right — knowing which stocks to hold and in what quantities — is the core problem this project solves.

The Problem

Index tracking

  • Given a $1B portfolio, which 40 stocks should we hold each quarter?
  • And how many shares of each, to best track (or beat) the index?
Strategy A — Cap Weighting
Weight each stock proportional to its market capitalisation. Simple, passive, mirrors the index directly.
Strategy B — Max Sharpe Ratio
Optimise weights to maximise risk-adjusted return. More complex, potentially higher performance.

The Efficient Frontier

Visualising risk vs. return

Every mix of stocks lands in risk-return space. The frontier is the best possible return for each level of risk. The Max Sharpe point is the steepest angle from the risk-free rate.

How Max Sharpe Ratio Works

Strategy B deep-dive

The core idea
Don't just chase returns — find the portfolio where you get the most return per unit of risk taken. The Sharpe Ratio measures exactly this.
Step 1 — Measure the past
Look at each stock's returns and volatility over the previous quarter. How much did it earn? How wild was the ride?
Step 2 — Find correlations
Some stocks move together (banks), some don't (luxury vs. energy). Mixing uncorrelated stocks reduces overall risk.
Step 3 — Optimise
Search for the exact weight combination that maximises:
Sharpe = Return − Risk-free rate ÷ Volatility
Result
Weights that aren't just proportional to size — they reflect the best risk-adjusted combination of all 40 stocks.

↓ next: how we make this optimisation reliable with limited data

Ledoit-Wolf Shrinkage

Making the optimisation reliable

The problem with limited data
We only have ~60 trading days of data per quarter. Estimating how 40 stocks relate to each other from 60 observations is noisy — like judging a coin's fairness from 10 flips.
Without shrinkage
The covariance matrix is taken at face value. Extreme values (one stock happened to spike) get treated as real signals. The optimiser over-bets on noise.
Analogy
One rainy Tuesday doesn't mean it rains every Tuesday. But a naive model would think so.
With Ledoit-Wolf
Pull extreme estimates back toward the average. Blend "what actually happened" with "a sensible baseline". Less sensitive to one quarter's fluke.
Result
A smoother, more stable covariance matrix → the optimiser finds weights that are likely to hold up next quarter, not just fit last quarter perfectly.

Cap Weight passive

  • Each stock's weight = its market cap ÷ total market cap of all 40
  • Larger companies get a higher weight — mirrors how the index itself is calculated
  • Simple, low-turnover, low cost to implement
  • Output: a CSV of 40 weights summing to 1.0

Max Sharpe Ratio Portfolio optimised

  • Sharpe ratio = return above the risk-free rate ÷ volatility — a measure of return per unit of risk
  • Uses historical returns to estimate expected return and covariance (how stocks move together)
  • Runs a numerical optimiser (scipy SLSQP) to find the weights that maximise the Sharpe ratio
  • Uses Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage (scikit-learn) to produce a more stable covariance estimate — avoids overfitting on short return histories
